The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is a large infrared telescope with a 6.5-meter primary mirror.
It was launched on December 25, 2021, and began science operations in July 2022.
JWST is a collaboration between NASA, the European Space Agency (ESA), and the Canadian Space Agency (CSA).

The telescope orbits the Sun at the second Lagrange point (L2), approximately 1.5 million kilometres from Earth.
Unlike the Hubble Space Telescope, which orbits Earth in low Earth orbit, JWST's L2 position allows it to
observe the universe in infrared light with minimal interference from Earth, Moon, and Sun.

JWST's four scientific goals are:
1. Search for the first galaxies or luminous objects formed after the Big Bang.
2. Determine how galaxies evolved from their formation until now.
3. Observe the formation of stars from the first stages to the formation of planetary systems.
4. Measure the physical and chemical properties of planetary systems and investigate the potential for life.

The telescope carries four primary instruments: NIRCam (Near Infrared Camera), NIRSpec (Near Infrared Spectrograph),
MIRI (Mid-Infrared Instrument), and FGS/NIRISS (Fine Guidance Sensor / Near InfraRed Imager and Slitless Spectrograph).

The five-layer sunshield is the size of a tennis court (21m x 14m) and keeps the instruments at around -233°C (40 K),
which is essential for detecting faint infrared signals. The primary mirror consists of 18 hexagonal gold-coated
beryllium segments that folded for launch and unfolded in space over a two-week deployment sequence.

In its first year of operations, JWST released stunning images of the Carina Nebula, Stephan's Quintet,
the Southern Ring Nebula, and the deepest infrared image of the universe ever taken (SMACS 0723).
